Most career advice in cybersecurity is either too vague to be useful or written by people trying to sell you something. This section is neither. It covers what entry level actually looks like, how people realistically get into the field, which certifications matter and which are marketing, and what to expect in terms of salary and progression.

## What Entry Level Actually Looks Like

The term entry level in cybersecurity job descriptions is notoriously misleading. You will see entry level roles requiring three to five years of experience, two or three certifications, and familiarity with a long list of enterprise tools. Most of those requirements are aspirational on the employer's side. They describe the ideal candidate, not the minimum viable one.

That said, entry level security roles are genuinely more demanding than entry level roles in many other fields. You are being trusted with access to sensitive systems and data. Employers want some evidence that you can be trusted with that access and that you have the technical foundation to do the job without constant supervision.

A Tier 1 SOC analyst spends most of their time reviewing alerts. Many alerts are false positives. The job at this level is pattern recognition, documentation, and knowing when something needs escalating. It is repetitive. That repetition is how you build the instincts that make you effective at Tier 2 and beyond. People who expect to be hunting advanced persistent threats on day one are usually disappointed.

A junior GRC analyst spends time on evidence collection, policy documentation, control mapping, and supporting audit preparation. The work is process-oriented and detail-heavy. It builds the risk and compliance knowledge that becomes very valuable as you progress.

A junior penetration tester often starts by running vulnerability scans, writing up findings, and supporting senior testers on engagements. The report writing is as important as the technical work and it takes time to develop.

Entry level security work is real work with real responsibilities. You are expected to contribute from day one, even if the contribution is modest at first.

## How People Actually Get In

There is no single path into cybersecurity. These are the routes that actually work.

**From IT.** The most common and most reliable path. Helpdesk, networking, sysadmin, or development experience gives you the technical foundation that security builds on. Many employers specifically look for IT background when hiring junior security staff because the baseline is already there.

**From a bootcamp or self-study.** Possible but harder without the IT foundation. The people who succeed with this route go deep on fundamentals, build home labs, and demonstrate practical skill rather than relying on certificates alone.

**From a degree.** Provides a solid foundation. The value varies by programme. Degrees help more with some employers than others. Government and large enterprises often value them. Fast-moving startups and MSSPs often care more about demonstrated skill.

**From adjacent fields.** Law enforcement, military, intelligence, and audit backgrounds have produced many successful security professionals. GRC in particular draws heavily from finance, law, and audit.

The common factor across all successful transitions is demonstrated practical knowledge. Employers want evidence that you can do something, not just that you have studied it.

## Home Labs

A home lab is a personal practice environment where you build, break, and experiment with security tools and techniques. It is one of the most effective ways to build practical skill outside of a job.

You do not need expensive hardware. A laptop with 8GB of RAM can run several virtual machines. Free cloud tiers from Azure, AWS, and Google Cloud let you build cloud environments without spending money if you are careful.

What you build in your home lab becomes part of your portfolio. A configured SIEM with documented detection rules, a penetration testing environment with notes on techniques you have practised, a simulated cloud tenant with access policies documented. These are things you can show and talk about in interviews.

## Certifications — What Actually Matters

**CompTIA Security+** is the most widely recognised entry level certification. It validates broad security knowledge rather than deep expertise. It appears in a huge number of job descriptions. It is a reasonable first certification but it is a floor, not a differentiator. Getting it does not make you stand out. Not having it at entry level is a disadvantage.

**BTL1** — Blue Team Labs Level 1. A practical, hands-on certification that requires completing real investigation tasks. Strong signal to employers because it is difficult to pass without genuine skill.

**OSCP** — Offensive Security Certified Professional. The gold standard for penetration testing. Entirely practical. 24 hours to compromise a lab environment and another 24 hours to write the report. Expensive, difficult, and highly respected. Build skills first. This is not a starting point.

**AZ-500** — Microsoft Azure Security Technologies. The primary Azure security certification. Relevant if you are targeting cloud security roles in Azure environments.

**CISSP** — the most widely recognised senior security certification. Requires five years of experience to sit. Better suited to mid-career professionals moving into senior or leadership roles than to people starting out.

**eJPT** — a good starting point for offensive security before OSCP. Practical, affordable, and a reasonable signal that you understand penetration testing basics.

Pick the certification most relevant to your target role. Study for it properly, not just to pass the exam. Then get practical experience to back it up. A certification with a home lab and practical portfolio is significantly more powerful than a certification alone.

## Salary Ranges

United Kingdom — entry level security roles typically start between £28,000 and £42,000. Mid-level professionals earn between £45,000 and £75,000. Senior and specialist roles reach £75,000 to £120,000 and beyond.

United States — entry level ranges from $55,000 to $85,000. Mid-level from $85,000 to $130,000. Senior from $120,000 to $200,000 and above in high-demand specialisations.

Europe — varies significantly by country. Germany, Netherlands, and Switzerland pay comparably to the UK. Eastern Europe pays considerably less but remote work for Western European employers is increasingly accessible.

Nigeria — entry level roles at Nigerian organisations typically pay between ₦150,000 and ₦400,000 monthly. Banking and fintech pay at the top end. Mid-level ranges from ₦400,000 to ₦900,000 monthly. Senior roles from ₦900,000 to ₦2.5 million monthly. Remote roles for UK or US employers often pay at international rates in dollars or pounds, which is significantly higher.

These figures are guides, not guarantees. Always check current market data before negotiating.

## Building Your Reputation

Getting your first security job is harder than getting your second. Before you have a job title, you need other evidence that you know what you are doing.

Write about what you learn. A blog, a LinkedIn post, a GitHub repository with documented projects. Explaining something clearly demonstrates that you understand it.

Participate in the community. Capture the flag competitions build skills and are recognised by employers. Bug bounty programmes let you practice offensive skills in legal, real-world environments. Open source security tool contributions demonstrate practical skill.

Build a portfolio of real work. The modules you complete on CyberPivot produce artifacts you can show. These are evidence of capability that a certificate alone cannot provide.

Network genuinely. Attend security meetups, conferences, and online communities. Most security jobs are filled through networks before they are ever advertised. OWASP local chapters, BSides conferences, and local security community groups exist in most cities including Lagos.

## What No One Tells You

Imposter syndrome is widespread in cybersecurity. The field moves fast, the knowledge required is broad, and there is always someone who knows more than you. Almost every security professional at every level occasionally feels like they do not know enough. That feeling does not go away. You learn to work with it.

The gap between certification knowledge and job reality is real. Passing a certification exam does not mean you are ready to do the job. Most people learn the actual job on the job. Certifications open doors. Experience is what you walk through them with.

Specialisation comes with time. Early in your career you need breadth. Specialisation into a specific area becomes more valuable as you progress and as you figure out what you actually enjoy doing.

The best security professionals are curious, honest about what they do not know, and willing to keep learning. The field rewards that combination more reliably than any certification.
