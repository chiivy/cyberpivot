Cybersecurity is the practice of protecting systems, networks, data, and people from attacks, damage, and unauthorised access. That is the textbook answer. Here is the more useful one.

Every organisation that uses computers has things worth protecting. Customer data. Financial records. Operational systems. Intellectual property. Reputation. When those things are stolen, destroyed, or disrupted, it costs money, trust, and sometimes lives. Cybersecurity is the discipline of preventing that from happening, and responding when it does anyway.

The scale of the problem is not abstract. In 2017, WannaCry ransomware took down 80 NHS trusts across the UK. Surgeries were cancelled. Ambulances were diverted. Patient records were inaccessible for days. All because of unpatched Windows systems and no offline backups. In 2020, the SolarWinds attack compromised over 18,000 organisations including the US Treasury, the Department of Homeland Security, and NATO. Attackers had been inside those networks for months before anyone noticed. In 2023, a ransomware attack on Royal Mail disrupted international postal services for weeks.

These are not edge cases. They are normal. Organisations of every size, in every sector, face threats constantly. The question is not whether they will be targeted. It is whether they will be ready.

## Why it exists

Cybersecurity exists because everything of value is connected to a network, and networks can be attacked. Thirty years ago most sensitive information lived in filing cabinets. Today it lives in databases accessible from anywhere in the world. That convenience created an attack surface that did not exist before.

The attackers are not all mysterious figures in dark rooms. Most cybercrime is organised and financially motivated. Criminal groups run ransomware operations like businesses, complete with revenue targets, affiliate programmes, and customer service for victims paying ransoms. Nation states run persistent campaigns against foreign governments and critical infrastructure. Insiders with legitimate access abuse it. Opportunists scan the internet constantly looking for easy targets.

The defenders are the people this platform trains.

## Why this career is worth pursuing

There are approximately 3.5 million unfilled cybersecurity jobs globally as of 2025. The UK alone has over 11,000 vacant positions. Every organisation that handles data needs security people, and most do not have enough.

This is not a temporary shortage. The threat landscape grows faster than the workforce. AI is changing how attacks are conducted. Cloud adoption has created entirely new attack surfaces. Regulations like GDPR, NIS2, and DORA are forcing organisations to take security seriously in ways they never did before.

Strong demand, competitive salaries, and genuine career progression exist for people who build real skills. The path is not easy. The opportunity is real.

## What the job actually involves

Most cybersecurity work is defensive. Monitoring, investigating, building controls, managing risk, writing policies, auditing systems. Offensive roles like penetration testing are well paid but represent a minority of the overall workforce.

A significant portion of the field is not deeply technical. GRC analysts, compliance officers, risk managers, and security awareness professionals manage frameworks, write policies, conduct audits, and help organisations understand their exposure. These roles are in high demand and accessible to people from non-technical backgrounds.

Tools help but they do not replace judgement. Knowing when an alert is a false positive, when a risk is acceptable, when an incident needs escalating, when to call the lawyers. That is human judgement and it is what employers are actually paying for.

Security teams work with IT, legal, HR, finance, and the board. Communication matters as much as technical skill at every level beyond entry.

## What employers expect at the door

When you walk into a cybersecurity interview at entry level, there are baseline concepts every employer assumes you understand. Not at expert depth. Well enough to speak clearly about them and apply them in context.

The rest of this Introduction covers those concepts. Not as a checklist to memorise. As a foundation to build on.

By the time you finish you will understand how attacks work, how defenders think, how the industry is structured, and what different roles actually do. That context is what makes everything in your chosen path make sense.
