Compliance is one of the most misunderstood areas of cybersecurity. People either dismiss it as box-ticking bureaucracy or treat it as the destination rather than a means to an end. Neither is right. Understanding what compliance actually is, why it exists, and how it relates to real security is essential knowledge for every security professional regardless of their role.

## Why Compliance Exists

Left to their own devices, most organisations will underinvest in security. The benefits of security are largely invisible when things are going well, and the costs are immediate and visible. Regulations exist to change that calculation.

Governments and industry bodies create compliance requirements to ensure that organisations handling sensitive data or operating critical systems meet a minimum standard of security. The minimum is rarely enough on its own, but it is better than nothing, and the financial penalties for non-compliance create an incentive that security risk arguments sometimes cannot.

GDPR fines can reach €20 million or 4% of global annual turnover, whichever is higher. The ICO fined British Airways £20 million following a 2018 data breach. PCI-DSS non-compliance can result in card brands refusing to process payments, which is an existential threat for most retail businesses. These consequences focus minds in ways that security presentations often cannot.

## The Difference Between Compliance and Security

Being compliant means you have met a defined set of requirements at a point in time. Being secure means you can actually protect your systems and data against real threats right now.

These are related but not the same thing. An organisation can pass every compliance audit and still be breached within weeks. The Target breach in 2013 happened while the company was PCI-DSS compliant. The attackers entered through a third-party HVAC vendor that had network access, exploited gaps the compliance checklist did not cover, and stole 40 million card numbers.

Compliance defines a floor, not a ceiling. Treating compliance as the goal rather than a baseline leads to organisations that tick boxes without building real capability. The best security programmes use compliance requirements as a starting point and build genuine security on top of them.

When you hear a security professional say "we are compliant" in response to a question about their security posture, that is a yellow flag. Compliance and security are not the same answer.

## The Major Frameworks and Regulations

There are a lot of frameworks and regulations in cybersecurity. Here is an honest overview of the ones that come up most often in practice.

**GDPR** — General Data Protection Regulation. EU law that applies to any organisation handling personal data of EU residents. Covers data subject rights, lawful basis for processing, breach notification within 72 hours, data protection by design, and restrictions on international data transfers. If you work for any organisation that has EU customers or employees, GDPR is relevant.

**UK GDPR** — the version of GDPR that applies in the UK following Brexit. Largely mirrors EU GDPR with some differences. Enforced by the Information Commissioner's Office.

**NDPR** — Nigeria Data Protection Regulation. Nigeria's equivalent of GDPR, enforced by the Nigeria Data Protection Commission. Applies to organisations processing personal data of Nigerian residents. Requires annual audits for data controllers of major importance.

**HIPAA** — Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act. US law protecting health information. Applies to healthcare providers, insurers, and their business associates.

**PCI-DSS** — Payment Card Industry Data Security Standard. Not a law but a contractual requirement imposed by card brands. Applies to any organisation that stores, processes, or transmits cardholder data. Version 4.0 is the current standard.

**SOX** — Sarbanes-Oxley Act. US law applying to publicly listed companies. Requires internal controls over financial reporting. IT General Controls are a significant component.

**ISO 27001** — the international standard for information security management systems. Applicable to any organisation of any size in any sector. The 2022 version is current. Certification is widely recognised globally.

**NIST CSF** — National Institute of Standards and Technology Cybersecurity Framework. Organised around six functions: Identify, Protect, Detect, Respond, Recover, and Govern. Version 2.0 released in 2024. Very common in US-aligned organisations and increasingly referenced globally.

**NIS2** — EU directive on network and information security. Came into force in 2023. Applies to essential and important entities across 18 sectors. Imposes incident reporting obligations, security requirements, and management accountability provisions.

**DORA** — Digital Operational Resilience Act. EU regulation for financial services. Full compliance required from January 2025. Covers ICT risk management, incident reporting, and third-party risk management.

**Cyber Essentials** — UK government-backed certification covering five basic security controls. Mandatory for UK government contracts.

**CBN Cybersecurity Framework** — mandatory for Nigerian banks and financial institutions. Issued by the Central Bank of Nigeria.

## Regulatory Bodies

**ICO** — Information Commissioner's Office. UK data protection regulator. Enforces UK GDPR.

**NDPC** — Nigeria Data Protection Commission. Enforces NDPR.

**FCA** — Financial Conduct Authority. UK financial services regulator.

**CBN** — Central Bank of Nigeria. Regulates Nigerian banking including cybersecurity requirements.

**SEC** — Securities and Exchange Commission. US financial markets regulator with cybersecurity disclosure requirements.

**CISA** — Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency. US government agency responsible for protecting critical infrastructure.

**NCC** — Nigerian Communications Commission. Regulates telecoms in Nigeria including cybersecurity requirements.

## Mapping Regulations to Your Role

Not every regulation is relevant to every job. Which ones matter depends on what industry you work in and where your organisation operates.

A security analyst working for a Nigerian fintech with UK and EU customers needs to understand NDPR, UK GDPR, GDPR, and PCI-DSS simultaneously. A GRC analyst at a US healthcare company needs HIPAA, SOX if the company is listed, and NIST CSF.

One of the most valuable skills a security professional can develop early is the ability to map an organisation's profile to the regulations that apply. This is covered in depth in the GRC path. But every security professional benefits from understanding the landscape well enough to ask the right questions.
