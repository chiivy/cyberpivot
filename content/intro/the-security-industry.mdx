Understanding what cybersecurity is and how attacks work is one thing. Understanding the industry you are walking into is another. Where do security people work? How are teams structured? What does a security department actually look like inside an organisation?

## How Security Fits Into a Business

Security exists to protect the organisation's ability to operate. That sounds obvious but it shapes everything about how security teams work.

Security is not the point of the business. A hospital's point is patient care. A bank's point is financial services. A retailer's point is selling things. Security enables those things to happen safely. That positioning matters because security professionals spend a lot of their time justifying spend, explaining risk in business terms, and negotiating with people who have other priorities.

A CISO presenting to the board is not talking about CVE scores and firewall rules. They are talking about operational risk, regulatory exposure, reputational damage, and financial impact. The ability to translate technical risk into business language is one of the most valuable skills at every level of a security career, and one of the most underdeveloped.

Security also does not operate in isolation. It works alongside IT, legal, HR, finance, and operations constantly. A data breach involves legal because of notification obligations. An insider threat investigation involves HR because of employment law. A new business system rollout involves security because someone needs to assess what risks it introduces. Security professionals who understand how these functions work and how to collaborate with them are significantly more effective than those who do not.

## In-House Security Teams

Most large organisations have internal security teams. The structure varies by size and maturity.

A small company might have one person responsible for everything security-related, or security might sit with the IT manager as an additional responsibility. There is no dedicated team, limited tooling, and usually reactive rather than proactive security practice.

A medium-sized organisation might have a small dedicated team. Usually a security manager or head of security, a few analysts, and someone handling compliance and risk. The team handles day-to-day monitoring, incident response, compliance requirements, and vendor assessments.

A large enterprise has a full security organisation. A CISO at the top. Separate teams for security operations, identity and access management, cloud security, application security, GRC, and sometimes a dedicated threat intelligence function. Each team has its own hierarchy from analysts to engineers to leads to managers.

Working in-house means deep familiarity with one environment. You learn the organisation's systems, its risk appetite, its quirks. The work is varied and you see the consequences of your decisions directly. The trade-off is that you are limited to one organisation's toolset and problems. Career progression can be slower if the team is small.

## MSSPs

A Managed Security Service Provider is a company that provides security services to other organisations. They run security operations centres, manage security tools, monitor environments, and respond to incidents on behalf of their clients.

Working at an MSSP means exposure to a huge variety of environments, industries, and attack types. You might be monitoring a bank in the morning and a manufacturing company in the afternoon. That variety accelerates learning significantly. The trade-off is that the work can be high volume and repetitive at the analyst level, and client constraints sometimes limit what you can do.

MSSPs are a common entry point into the industry because they hire at scale and the volume of work builds skills quickly. Many experienced practitioners started at MSSPs and moved into in-house roles later.

## Consultancies

Security consultancies provide advisory, assessment, and project-based services. Penetration testing firms, GRC consulting practices, incident response firms, and advisory practices that help organisations build security programmes all fall here.

Consulting work means moving between clients and projects. One month you are conducting a penetration test for a financial institution. The next you are helping a healthcare organisation prepare for an ISO 27001 audit. The variety is high and the learning curve is steep in the best way.

The trade-off is that consulting is demanding. Deadlines are client-driven. Building deep knowledge of any single environment is harder when you are always moving. Senior consultants and partners in established firms earn well. The typical path goes from junior consultant through senior consultant to manager or principal, often with the option to specialise or move into business development.

## Red, Blue, and Purple Teams

The industry uses colour coding to describe different security functions. Three of these are universally recognised and you will encounter them constantly.

**Red Team** simulates real adversaries in authorised, controlled attacks. A red team engagement goes beyond finding vulnerabilities. It emulates how a specific type of attacker would operate, with stealth, persistence, and achieving defined objectives as the measures of success. Red teamers need deep offensive skill and usually have years of penetration testing experience first. This is a relatively small part of the overall security workforce.

**Blue Team** is the defenders. Analysts monitoring for threats, investigating incidents, hunting for attackers, and building detection capabilities. Most security professionals work in blue team functions for most of their careers. If you are going into SOC, incident response, threat hunting, or detection engineering, you are blue team.

**Purple Team** is not a permanent team in most organisations. It is a collaborative exercise where red and blue work together in real time. The red team attacks, the blue team tries to detect it, and both immediately share what happened and why. The goal is to improve detection capability faster than either team could achieve working separately. Purple teaming is increasingly common because traditional red team engagements where the report arrives months later and half the findings are already remediated are not as efficient as they could be.

You may also hear **White Team** mentioned in the context of formal security exercises. The white team acts as referee. They define rules of engagement, monitor that the red team stays within scope, and document outcomes. Usually senior leaders or external observers.

Some communities use additional colours to describe developer and executive security functions. These are useful conceptual descriptions in certain contexts but not standardised industry terms. You may encounter them. Do not expect everyone you work with to use them.

## The CISO and Security Leadership

The Chief Information Security Officer is responsible for the organisation's overall security posture. They report to the CEO, CTO, or board depending on the organisation. Their job is strategic and communication-heavy. Managing the security programme, reporting risk to leadership, managing budget, hiring and developing the team, and representing security in business decisions.

Most CISOs have fifteen to twenty years of security experience across multiple disciplines. Some come from technical backgrounds and moved into leadership. Others come from risk and compliance. The role requires both technical credibility and business communication skill.

## How Security Decisions Get Made

Security teams do not operate with unlimited authority. They advise, assess, and recommend. Business leaders make the final call on risk acceptance.

A security team might identify that a legacy system has a serious vulnerability but cannot be patched without taking it offline for a week, which would cost significant revenue. They assess the risk, propose compensating controls, and present options. A business leader decides whether to accept the risk, implement the mitigations, or take the downtime. That is risk acceptance and it happens constantly.

Security is not about eliminating all risk. No organisation can do that. It is about understanding risk well enough to make informed decisions about what to accept, what to mitigate, and what to avoid. Security professionals who understand business constraints and can communicate risk in business terms are more effective than those who treat every vulnerability as a critical emergency.

## What Employers Are Actually Looking For

Beyond the technical skills specific to each role, almost every security employer values the same broader qualities.

Curiosity. Security changes constantly. People who are genuinely curious about how things work learn faster and stay current without it feeling like work.

Communication. Writing clear incident reports. Explaining risk to a non-technical manager. Presenting findings to a client. These are daily activities in most security roles and they separate good practitioners from great ones.

Judgement. Knowing when an alert is worth escalating. When a risk is acceptable. When to call in outside help. Tools help but they do not replace the ability to make good decisions with incomplete information under pressure.

Collaboration. Security does not happen in isolation. The ability to work with people across IT, legal, business operations, and leadership is not optional at any level.

The technical skills get you in the room. These qualities determine how far you go.
