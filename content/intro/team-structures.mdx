Knowing that security teams exist is one thing. Understanding how they actually function day to day, how the different roles interact, and where decisions get made is what helps you navigate your first job and plan your career intelligently.

## The Security Operations Centre

The SOC is where most people start in defensive security. It is the function responsible for monitoring, detecting, and responding to security events in real time.

A mature SOC operates around the clock in shifts. The team is usually structured in tiers.

Tier 1 analysts handle the alert queue. They review alerts, make an initial assessment of whether something is real or noise, and escalate confirmed threats to Tier 2. The volume of alerts in a busy SOC is high. Alert fatigue is a real problem that well-run SOCs actively manage through detection tuning and automation.

Tier 2 analysts investigate escalated alerts in depth. They have more experience, more context, and more tools. They determine the scope and severity of an incident and decide whether to invoke the incident response process.

Tier 3 and above handles the most complex investigations, threat hunting, and detection engineering. These are senior practitioners who define how the SOC works, build detection capabilities, and handle incidents that require deep expertise.

Detection engineers write and maintain the rules that generate alerts. The quality of the detection rule set determines the quality of the entire SOC. Bad rules create noise. Missing rules mean attacks go undetected.

Threat hunters work proactively. Rather than waiting for an alert, they form a hypothesis about how an attacker might be operating and go looking for evidence. Threat hunting is typically a senior role that follows several years of SOC experience.

## The Incident Response Team

IR kicks in when something has been confirmed as a real incident. A ransomware attack. A confirmed data breach. Evidence of an attacker who has been inside the environment for weeks. The IR team investigates what happened, contains the damage, removes the attacker, and recovers affected systems.

IR work is high pressure and time sensitive. During an active incident the team is working long hours, often without complete information, making containment decisions that could either stop an attack or cause disruption to business operations if they get it wrong. Documentation during an incident is as important as the technical response because everything becomes evidence.

Post-incident reviews are where the real learning happens. What did the attacker do? How did they get in? What did we detect and what did we miss? A good post-incident review produces concrete improvements to detection rules, playbooks, and controls.

## The GRC Function

GRC sits differently from operations. It is less about daily monitoring and more about programme management, risk oversight, and compliance assurance.

A GRC function maintains the organisation's risk register, coordinates compliance activities, writes and maintains security policies, manages vendor risk assessments, prepares for audits, and reports the organisation's security posture to leadership.

GRC professionals interact with almost every part of the business. IT, legal, HR, finance, and the board. The work is less reactive than SOC work and more programme-driven. Annual cycles around audits and certification renewals. Ongoing activities around risk register maintenance and vendor assessments. Project activities when new regulations come into force.

GRC roles often have significant influence over security investment decisions because they own the risk register and the framework that justifies security spend.

## The Cloud Security Team

Cloud security teams secure the organisation's cloud environments. Configuring identity and access management, network controls, monitoring, and compliance posture. Reviewing infrastructure code before it is deployed to catch misconfigurations before they reach production. Investigating cloud-specific incidents.

Cloud security engineers often work closely with DevOps and platform engineering teams. The speed at which cloud infrastructure changes means security has to be embedded in the development and deployment process rather than reviewing things after the fact.

## The Application Security Team

AppSec teams work at the intersection of security and software development. Their job is making sure applications are built securely from the start rather than finding vulnerabilities after they are in production.

An AppSec engineer might spend their day reviewing pull requests for security issues, running automated scanning tools, facilitating a threat modelling session with a development team, triaging vulnerability reports from bug bounty researchers, or updating secure coding guidelines.

AppSec work requires understanding both security and software development. You need to read code comfortably, understand how web applications work at a technical level, and communicate findings to developers in a way that makes sense to them.

## How Teams Interact

In a mature organisation these functions feed into each other constantly.

The SOC detects an anomaly and raises an incident. The IR team takes over. Their findings reveal a vulnerability in a web application. AppSec is engaged to understand the root cause and remediate it. The cloud security team assesses whether the same vulnerability exists elsewhere. GRC updates the risk register and coordinates regulatory notification if personal data was involved. Leadership receives a briefing.

That chain of handoffs only works when teams understand each other's work, communicate clearly, and have processes that connect their activities. Organisations where these functions operate in silos are significantly less effective.

## Career Paths Within Each Function

From SOC the natural progression is Tier 1 to Tier 2 to Tier 3 or detection engineering or threat hunting. From there some people move into incident response, security architecture, or management.

From GRC the natural progression is analyst to senior analyst to GRC manager to head of GRC or CISO track. Some GRC professionals move into consulting where they work across multiple organisations.

From AppSec the natural progression is engineer to senior engineer to lead to head of application security or security architecture.

From cloud security the progression is engineer to senior engineer to cloud security architect. The market for senior cloud security expertise is strong and salaries reflect that.

## Your First Role

Your first security role is your entry point, not your destination. The goal is to get in, build real experience, and use that foundation to get somewhere better.

For most people breaking in, the priority is simple. Get relevant experience on your CV. Learn as much as you can while you are there. Then move when you have something to move with.

Take what you can get to start. Then look for better once you have something to show. Most people's second security role is significantly better than their first, and the first role is what made the second one possible.

## Career Paths Are Not Fixed

The progressions described here are the most common routes, not the only ones. Careers in cybersecurity are rarely linear.

People move from SOC into cloud security. From GRC into consulting. From penetration testing into product security. From incident response into threat intelligence. From technical roles into management and back again.

Some people combine disciplines in ways that create entirely new roles. The paths here give you a starting point and a direction. Where you end up depends on what you enjoy, what you are good at, what opportunities arise, and what the market needs. Stay curious, keep building skills, and do not assume the path you start on is the only one available to you.
